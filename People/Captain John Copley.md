# Captain John Copley (c. 1610–post 1657)

## Biographical Profile

- **Birth:** c. 1610, Worcestershire, England (exact date unknown)
- **Death:** post-1657, Ireland (date and place unknown)
- **Status:** Younger son of [[People/Thomas Copley Jr.|Thomas Copley Jr.]] of Bredon's Norton, Worcestershire
- **Occupation:** Military officer, entrepreneur, ironmaster
- **Wife:** [[#Family Relationships|Margaret, daughter of George Newport of Rushock]]
- **Known residences:** Worcestershire (1610–1634), Italy (1634), Kingswood Forest near Bristol (1656–57), Youghal, County Cork (1651), Ireland (post-1657)

## Notable Life Events

**1634 — In Italy:** Documented in the [[Topics/Captain John Copley Research|1634 Visitation of Worcestershire]] as being "in Italy." Likely studying Italian iron-smelting techniques to support his later entrepreneurial ventures.

**1642 — Parliamentary Petition:** Petitioned the House of Commons concerning "some strange brave Exploits he would undertake in Ireland" during the Irish Rebellion of 1641. His petition was referred to the Lieutenant of the Tower and the Lieutenant of the Ordnance, but no record of acceptance survives. (Source: *Journal of the House of Commons*, Vol. 2)

**1651 — Clerk of the Market, Youghal:** Served as Clerk of the Market in Youghal, County Cork — historically significant as the Irish seat of Sir Walter Raleigh. His presence in Youghal is circumstantially consistent with family network ties to Raleigh's circle. (Source: Richard Caulfield, ed., *Council Book of the Corporation of Youghal*)

**1656–1657 — Kingswood Ironworks:** Obtained a Cromwellian patent to produce iron using coal rather than charcoal. Established an iron-making venture at Kingswood Forest, near Bristol (~40 miles from Bredon's Norton). Engaged Dud Dudley to improve the design of his bellows. The venture failed, and Copley subsequently moved to Ireland in 1657. (Source: Dud Dudley, *Mettallum Martis*, 1665, pp. 20–21)

**Post-1657 — Ireland:** Emigrated to Ireland; further records do not survive. A man with Cromwellian patent experience and engineering knowledge arriving in 1657 as the land settlement was being administered would have been well-positioned to receive a grant, but no specific record has yet been found.
