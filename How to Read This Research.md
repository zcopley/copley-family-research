# How to Read This Research

This site is a living genealogical research project, not a finished family history. It combines family narrative, person profiles, source tracking, open research questions, and working hypotheses. Some claims are well documented; others are active leads that are useful but not yet proven.

## Evidence Labels

The project uses confidence labels so readers can separate documented fact from useful but unfinished research.

| Label | How To Read It |
|---|---|
| **Verified** | Supported by a named primary source, source image, record extract, or clearly identified contemporary source. |
| **Strong working conclusion** | Multiple independent clues point the same way, but the direct identity record is still missing. |
| **Plausible** | A reasonable synthesis or family-memory interpretation, but not yet proven. |
| **Oral / family tradition** | Preserved in family memory or family-authored material; useful, but should be corroborated before treated as fact. |
| **Unresolved** | Important question remains open. |
| **Contradicted / rejected** | Earlier claim is contradicted by later evidence and should not be repeated as fact. |

When in doubt, check [[Sources and Evidence Index]] before relying on a claim.

## The Core Story

The central line begins with [[People/Michael Copley Sr|Michael Copley Sr.]], born around 1813 and associated by family tradition with [[Places/Kilgefin Ireland|Kilgefin, County Roscommon]], and [[People/Ann Copley|Ann Copley]], remembered as Ann Munday but now treated as likely Murray pending direct proof. Their family settled in [[Places/Lewis County West Virginia|Lewis County, West Virginia]], where land, Catholic community formation, Irish migration networks, and later oil development shaped the family's American story.

The most developed 19th- and 20th-century line runs through [[People/John Copley|John Copley]] and [[People/Mary Ellen Dolan Copley|Mary Ellen Dolan Copley]], then into the Sardo, Ruland, Stephen Copley, and Tom Copley branches.

## Reading Rule

Read the narrative for the story, but use the evidence index to decide how much weight to give a claim. A page can be valuable even when it is not final: in genealogy, a clearly labeled unresolved lead is often the thing that makes the next discovery possible.
