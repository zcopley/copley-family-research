RQ-M1 INTERPRETATION

Does John Byrne add another Ireland-born household on image 209?
Yes, confirmed. John Byrne (52, Ireland) and Ellen Byrne (45, Ireland) form a second Ireland-born household on image 209, in addition to Thomas Casey (47, Ireland) and Mary Casey (40, Ireland). Elisabeth Byrne (17, Maryland) was born in Maryland, not Ireland. The Byrne household is a complete Ireland-born head-of-household unit (both spouses born in Ireland). This is directly confirmed from both the written image and the FamilySearch index.

Is the Carey household Ireland-born, Irish-American, or unrelated?
This is the most significant finding: The "8-person Carey household" on image 210 is not an independent household. It is the continuation of Thomas and Mary Casey's household (household 8) across the page break between images 209 and 210. The eight children enumerated under the surname "Carey" are -- in the index -- all within the same household as the Ireland-born parents Thomas and Mary Casey.

- None of the Carey children are listed as born in Ireland. The four oldest were born in Virginia; the four youngest in West Virginia.
- Whether the surname discrepancy (parents = Casey, children = Carey) reflects an enumerator variant or a genuine different surname cannot be determined from the index alone. It requires direct, high-resolution examination of the handwriting on images 209-210.
- Conservatively stated: The "Carey" entries on image 210 represent, at minimum, the children of Ireland-born parents (Thomas and Mary Casey, both Ireland). They are Irish-American by parentage, not Ireland-born themselves.

Does this create a small Casey / Byrne / Carey Irish cluster in Willey?
Yes, with qualification. On images 209-210, Willey district, there are at minimum two Ireland-born households:
1. John Byrne (Ireland) + Ellen Byrne (Ireland) -- a self-contained Ireland-born couple
2. Thomas Casey (Ireland) + Mary Casey (Ireland) -- Ireland-born parents with a large family of Virginia/West Virginia-born children

These are on the same two census pages and in the same enumeration district, suggesting a small but real Irish presence in Willey. Whether this constitutes a geographic "cluster" or is coincidental proximity within the enumerator's route is not determinable from the census alone.

Does this change the interpretation of Willey compared with Battelle?
That comparison is beyond scope per the research rules, and I have not examined any Battelle data. Stated conservatively on the Willey evidence alone: Willey has at least two Ireland-born households confirmed on images 209-210, giving a small Irish presence, not a large one. The "Carey" children extend the Casey family numerically but do not add Ireland-born birthplaces. The Byrne household is an independent Ireland-born unit.
