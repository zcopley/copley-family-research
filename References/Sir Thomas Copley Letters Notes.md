## Access Status

The Google Books record identifies the source, but I did not find full text available online. Treat this as a high-priority source to obtain through a research library, interlibrary loan, or a Roxburghe Club holding.

## Why It Matters

This is likely the best edited source for the recusant Sir Thomas Copley's own correspondence with Queen Elizabeth and her ministers. It matters because it is much closer to the underlying records than later family-history summaries.

## Usable Details From Accessible References

- The [Dictionary of National Biography entry for Sir Thomas Copley](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Dictionary_of_National_Biography%2C_1885-1900/Copley%2C_Thomas) says Sir Thomas Copley of Gatton left England without license around 1570 and corresponded with Cecil, other Elizabethan ministers, and sometimes the queen, seeking pardon and permission to return and enjoy his estates.
- The DNB says his title of baron and his letters of marque against the Dutch were subjects of this correspondence, and that much of the material survives in State Papers and Cottonian, Lansdowne, and Harleian manuscripts.
- A 2025 Cambridge article on English Catholic seamen in Spanish service uses Christie as a key source for Copley's Spanish-service period. It says Copley was recruiting soldiers and mariners for Spanish service by 1574, received letters of marque against Dutch shipping, and later left the Netherlands for Paris around early 1580.
- The same article notes that Copley framed his activity as service compatible with loyalty to monarchy, but Elizabeth's government did not accept that argument because English policy supported the Dutch rebels.
- *Annals of an Old Manor-House: Sutton Place, Guildford* uses the Copley/Weston context to say that a 1569 letter from Sir Thomas Copley to Sir Henry Weston asked for protection under persecution, and that Mary Copley later brought the Gatton estates to the Weston family through her 1637 marriage to John Weston.
- Historic England's listing for Leigh Place confirms the house is a Grade II* listed building in Leigh, Surrey, and says it was remodelled and extended in the seventeenth century by Margaret Copeley.

## Research Implications

- Strengthens the claim that Sir Thomas Copley was not merely a passive exile: he remained in political correspondence over his status, title, and property.
- Supports Tom Copley's broader point that the Gatton line retained legal and family continuity after recusant exile, though the exact estate mechanism still needs direct record work.
- Corroborates the Weston transfer in broad terms, but the accessible published version differs from Tom's oral note: it identifies Mary Copley, great-granddaughter of Sir Thomas, as the person who brought Gatton to the Westons by marrying John Weston in 1637.
- Gives a better route for archival follow-up: Christie points toward the Record Office / State Papers and British Museum manuscript collections, not only local Surrey estate papers.
