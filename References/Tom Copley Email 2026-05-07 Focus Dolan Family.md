The Patrick Lead: Although the 1824 Tithe only lists "John," it was common for multiple generations (like a father named Patrick and a son named John) to live on the same plot, with only the eldest or the lease-holder listed for tax purposes. [1 (https://www.facebook.com/groups/RoscommonIrelandGenealogy/posts/6495561883821122/), 2 (https://www.billmacafee.com/valuationrecords/readmoregriffval.htm)]

Estate Context: Much of this land was part of the Pakenham Mahon (Strokestown House) Estate. Their Tenant Rentals (https://www.facebook.com/groups/RoscommonIrelandGenealogy/posts/10013975661979709/) often provide more granular detail than the Tithes, sometimes listing sub-tenants or "cottiers" who worked the land but didn't pay the tithe directly. [1 (https://www.facebook.com/groups/RoscommonIrelandGenealogy/posts/10013975661979709/)]

Why these townlands matter

These areas are within walking distance of the townlands where the Copley family was established. The close proximity of Carrowintoher and Carroward to known Copley holdings confirms that these two families were near neighbors, which was the most common driver for marriages like the one between John and Sarah.

The Strokestown Park Archive (also known as the Pakenham-Mahon Papers) is one of the most significant resources for tracing your Dolan ancestors, as it contains over 55,000 original documents including tenant rentals, maps, and estate management notes that bridge the gap where church records are missing. Key Evidence for Dolans of Kilgefin

Records within this archive and related 19th-century sources pinpoint exactly where the Dolans who likely parented Sarah lived:

Identified Tenants: As of 1824, John Dolan is recorded as a primary householder in the townlands of Carrowintoher, Carroward, and Kilmacough within the Parish of Kilgefin. These lands were held under the Mahon estate. [1 (https://landedestates.ie/estate/1463), 2 (http://titheapplotmentbooks.nationalarchives.ie/search/tab/results.jsp?surname=Dolan&firstname=John&county=&townland=&parish=&search=Search&sort=&pageSize=&pager.offset=70)]

Rental Rolls: The Strokestown Park House Archive (https://strokestownpark.ie/treasures-of-the-strokestown-famine-archive/) holds rentals dating back to the early 1800s. These often list "lives" on a lease—naming a father, son, or even a daughter (like Sarah) to determine the lease’s duration. [1 (https://catalogue.nli.ie/Record/vtls000512345/TOC), 2 (https://www.facebook.com/groups/RoscommonIrelandGenealogy/posts/10013975661979709/)]

Proximity to Copleys: These townlands are directly adjacent to the Ballagh area where the Copleys were established, reinforcing that they were immediate neighbors.

Recommended Research Strategy

Since you are looking for Sarah Dolan's parents (likely Patrick and Bridget based on naming patterns), you should focus on these specific collections:

Strokestown Tenant Census (1847): While later than Michael’s emigration, this census (Document 127 in the archive) lists 12,000 occupants across 68 townlands. It may name siblings of Sarah (like the William who stayed behind) and their exact holdings in Kilmacough or Carrowintoher. [1 (https://strokestownpark.ie/treasures-of-the-strokestown-famine-archive/)]

National Library of Ireland (NLI) Deeds: Look for Marriage Settlements in the Pakenham-Mahon Papers (MS 48,355) (https://www.nli.ie/sites/default/files/2022-12/174_pakenham-mahon-papers-collection-list.pdf). Landlords often required a legal deed for a marriage to ensure the tenant's land wasn't subdivided, which would name the bride's father. [1 (https://www.nli.ie/sites/default/files/2022-12/174_pakenham-mahon-papers-collection-list.pdf), 2 (https://catalogue.nli.ie/Collection/vtls000838331/CollectionList?filter%5B%5D=geographic_facet%3A%22Ireland%22&filter%5B%5D=topic_facet%3A%22Wills%22&hiddenFilters%5B%5D=hierarchy_top_id%3A%22vtls000838331%22&hiddenFilters%5B%5D=%21id%3A%22vtls000838331%22&type=AllFields)]

Registry of Deeds (Dublin): If the Dolans were substantial "strong farmers," their marriage or land transfers might be registered in the Registry of Deeds, which covers the 1790–1810 period not found in church registers.
