## Major Findings

- **1870 strengthens the broader Irish-cluster model, not a Murray-centered model.** The original 1870 pass did not find an Ireland-born Murray / Murry household in Lewis County, but the later Murry continuity follow-up corrected that negative result: adult Patrick Murray appears in Sheridan / Lewis County in 1870 under a record FamilySearch partially misindexed as Lincoln County. Luke Murray had already moved to Rhode Island.
- **Battelle district is the strongest 1870 Irish farming cluster.** Owen Mulooly appears with an established farm family, and Patrick Dolan appears in the Patrick Ferril household.
- **Willey district has a smaller Irish farming cluster.** Thomas Hanrahan and Thomas / Bridget Cummings are sequential or near-sequential Ireland-born households.
- **Follow-up image review confirmed the cluster is larger than the surname search showed.** Battelle page 4 / image 8 contains at least four Ireland-born farming heads: Michael Bush, Michael Leely, Owen Mulooly, and Michael McDonald. This corrects the earlier Fish / Foley-Riley / unread readings.
- **The supposed extra Willey image 265 Ireland-born household was a false read.** The lines before Thomas Cummings are the Virginia-born Jasper Butcher household, not an additional Ireland-born farmer.
- **Battelle and Willey tie to the Weston postal area.** Image 45 confirms `Weston, W. Va.` for Battelle; image 265 confirms `Weston, W. Va.` for Willey.
- **District geography currently favors a Weston / courthouse-hinterland reading.** The later district-mapping pass found that 1850 District 30, the 1860 blank rural pages, and 1870 Battelle point more directly to Weston than to Cove Lick / Camden / Loveberry.
- **The Copley / Coopley absence continues.** No Copley farming household appears in 1870; Nancy Coplin in Weston appears to be an institutional resident and is not treated as a Copley match.
- **Cummings is now confirmed as relevant.** The 1860 Cummings / Cummons ambiguity resolves into an Ireland-born Thomas and Bridget Cummings household in Willey by 1870.
- **The Mary Casey lead resolves into a Thomas / Mary Casey household.** Thomas and Mary Casey are both Ireland-born in Willey, with a Virginia-born son John, adding a small family household to the Willey Irish inventory.
- **The Byrne / Carey follow-up confirms another Ireland-born Willey couple and corrects the Carey lead.** John and Ellen Byrne on image 209 were born in Ireland. The apparent image 210 Carey household appears to be the continuation of the Thomas / Mary Casey household across the page break rather than a separate household.
- **The image-capture pass strengthens Battelle as the strongest 1870 Irish farming cluster.** It confirms image-read evidence for Bush, Leely, Mulooly, Ferril / Dolan, Hanrahan, Cummings, Byrne, and Casey / Carey, and it identifies a FamilySearch index error: Owen Mulooly's birthplace is indexed as Virginia but the image reads Ireland.
- **The Murry continuity pass resolves the 1860 Murry gap.** Luke Murray was in North Providence, Rhode Island, by 1870; adult Patrick Murray remained in Lewis County at Sheridan and died in Lewis County in 1871.
- **Ireland-born Whites disappear from the 1870 Lewis County index.** This creates a second continuity gap beside the vanished 1860 Murry brothers.

## Interpretation for RQ-M1

The 1870 pass still **weakens a Murray-specific anchor-family reading**, but the reason is now clearer. The 1860 household head Luke Murray had moved to Rhode Island before the 1870 census, while the separate adult Patrick Murray remained in Lewis County but was hidden from the first pass by a Sheridan / Lewis event-place indexing problem. Patrick's presence gives local Murry continuity, but not pre-1843 anchor evidence.

It **strengthens the broader Irish Catholic settlement-cluster model** by identifying rural Ireland-born farming clusters in **Battelle** and **Willey**. The strongest visible 1870 names are Bush, Leely, Mulooly, McDonald, Ferril / Dolan, Hanrahan, and Cummings, not Murray.

The persistent absence of Copley / Coopley households in both 1860 and 1870 remains a significant unresolved gap. It may indicate migration away, death, unrecognized indexing, a district mapping issue, or another record problem.
