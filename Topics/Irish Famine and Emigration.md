# Irish Famine and Emigration

## Historical Context
The Great Famine (1840s-1850s) was the most dramatic emigration driver in 19th-century Ireland, but outmigration pressures began earlier and continued long after the famine peak. Rural families in western counties, including Roscommon and nearby regions, faced tenancy insecurity, food vulnerability, and limited economic mobility.

For this project, famine-era context matters because it explains:
- Why Irish households accepted high-risk migration pathways.
- How chain migration and kin networks accelerated relocation.
- Why labor corridors in U.S. port and rail regions became common first destinations.

## Timeline
- **1820s-1830s**: High tenancy pressure and agrarian instability in western Ireland.
- **1837-1838**: Reported Copley-linked U.S. arrivals (Patrick/Bridget, then Michael as “Copely”).
- **1845-1852**: Great Famine years intensify transatlantic migration from Irish counties.
- **1840s-1850s**: Expansion of Irish labor corridors into Mid-Atlantic rail and settlement zones.
- **By mid/late 19th century**: Established Copley household rooted in Lewis County.
